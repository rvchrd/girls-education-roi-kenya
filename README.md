# Girls' Education ROI Model - Kenya

## Project Overview
This project is a financial model built in Google Sheets that estimates the 
lifetime return on investment (ROI) of one additional year of secondary 
education for a girl in rural Kenya. The model applies a discounted cash 
flow (DCF) framework to compare the costs of schooling with projected 
increases in lifetime earnings.

The project was developed to connect my background in girls' education 
advocacy through the Msichana Bora Initiative in Kitui County with 
practical financial analysis methods commonly used in development 
finance and investment analysis.

## Data Sources and Assumptions
The model draws on a combination of academic research and development 
finance literature, including:

1. Psacharopoulos, G., & Patrinos, H. A. (2018). Returns to investment in 
   education: A decennial review of the global literature. *Education 
   Economics*, 26(5), 445–458.
2. World Bank research on the economic returns to girls' education and 
   human capital investment.
3. Owen Ozier's regression discontinuity study on secondary schooling 
   outcomes in Kenya (World Bank Policy Research Working Paper 7384, 2015).

Baseline annual income and schooling cost figures are working estimates 
based on typical rural Kenyan household income levels and public 
secondary school fee structures. These are flagged as estimates and are 
intended to be refined with detailed Kenya National Bureau of Statistics 
(KNBS) Labour Force Survey data in future iterations of this model.

## Methodology
1. Estimate the direct cost of four years of secondary schooling, 
   including tuition, uniforms, books, and related expenses.
2. Project lifetime earnings with and without additional secondary 
   education.
3. Apply an 8% discount rate over a 35-year working life to calculate 
   the present value of future earnings gains.
4. Compute ROI as the ratio of discounted net benefits to total 
   schooling costs.



## Key Findings

In the base-case scenario, completing four years of secondary education 
generates an estimated 335% return on investment. Under assumptions that 
include higher wage premiums associated with girls' education  consistent 
with findings that women experience above-average returns to schooling 
the estimated ROI rises to approximately 420%.

These results suggest that girls' secondary education can be one of the 
highest-return investments available in development finance, with 
substantial long-term economic benefits for individuals, households, 
and communities.
